# Modern Art Movements: From Impressionism to Abstract Expressionism

## Introduction

Modern art (roughly 1860-1970) encompasses diverse movements that challenged traditional representation, exploring new subjects, techniques, and philosophies. These movements reflect broader social, technological, and intellectual changes.

## Impressionism (1860s-1880s)

### Characteristics

- Capture fleeting effects of light and atmosphere
- Outdoor painting (en plein air)
- Visible brushstrokes
- Everyday subjects: leisure, urban life, landscapes
- Rejection of academic conventions

### Key Artists

**Claude Monet (1840-1926):**
- "Impression, Sunrise" (1872): Named the movement
- Serial paintings: Haystacks, Rouen Cathedral, Water Lilies
- Pure color, dissolution of form

**Pierre-Auguste Renoir (1841-1919):**
- Joyful scenes of Parisian life
- Dappled light on figures
- Sensuous brushwork

**Edgar Degas (1834-1917):**
- Ballet dancers, café scenes
- Asymmetrical compositions influenced by photography
- Pastel technique

### Context

- Japanese print influence (ukiyo-e)
- Paint tube invention enabled outdoor work
- Rising middle class as subject and audience
- Photography freed painting from documentation

## Post-Impressionism (1880s-1900s)

### Diverse Responses

Artists extending beyond Impressionism in individual directions.

**Paul Cézanne (1839-1906):**
- "Treat nature by the cylinder, the sphere, the cone"
- Multiple viewpoints in single image
- Structural analysis of form
- Father of modern art

**Vincent van Gogh (1853-1890):**
- Emotional color: "Starry Night"
- Energetic brushwork
- Symbolic use of color
- 900 paintings in 10 years

**Paul Gauguin (1848-1903):**
- Flat areas of color (cloisonnism)
- Tahitian subjects
- Primitivism and spiritual themes

**Georges Seurat (1859-1891):**
- Pointillism: Systematic color dots
- "Sunday on La Grande Jatte"
- Scientific approach to optics

## Expressionism (1905-1920s)

### Die Brücke (The Bridge)

German group emphasizing raw emotion:
- **Ernst Ludwig Kirchner:** Angular figures, urban alienation
- **Emil Nolde:** Religious themes, vivid color
- Woodcut revival
- Primitive art influence

### Der Blaue Reiter (The Blue Rider)

Munich-based, more spiritual:
- **Wassily Kandinsky:** Path to abstraction
- **Franz Marc:** Animals as spiritual subjects
- Color theory and synesthesia

### Characteristics

- Distorted form for emotional effect
- Bold, non-naturalistic color
- Psychological intensity
- Response to industrialization and war

## Fauvism (1904-1908)

"Wild beasts" (les fauves) of color:

**Henri Matisse (1869-1954):**
- "The Joy of Life," "Dance"
- Pure, expressive color
- Decorative flatness
- Cut-outs in late work

**André Derain:**
- Vivid landscapes
- Color as independent element

## Cubism (1907-1914)

### Revolutionary Approach

**Analytic Cubism (1907-1912):**
- Fragment and analyze form
- Multiple simultaneous viewpoints
- Monochromatic palette (browns, grays)
- "Les Demoiselles d'Avignon" (Picasso, 1907)

**Synthetic Cubism (1912-1914):**
- Collage: Paper, newspaper, wallpaper
- Brighter colors
- Signs and symbols
- Reconstruction rather than analysis

### Key Artists

**Pablo Picasso (1881-1973):**
- Most influential 20th-century artist
- Multiple style periods
- "Guernica" (1937): Anti-war masterpiece

**Georges Braque (1882-1963):**
- Collaborated with Picasso
- Musical instrument themes
- Papier collé innovation

## Futurism (1909-1944)

Italian movement celebrating:
- Speed, technology, dynamism
- Rejection of the past
- Machine aesthetic

**Umberto Boccioni:**
- "Unique Forms of Continuity in Space" (sculpture)
- Motion and energy

**Giacomo Balla:**
- "Dynamism of a Dog on a Leash"
- Sequential motion

## Dada (1916-1924)

### Anti-Art Movement

Born in Zurich, spread to Berlin, Paris, New York:
- Response to World War I absurdity
- Chance operations
- Collage and photomontage
- Performance and provocation

### Key Figures

**Marcel Duchamp (1887-1968):**
- "Fountain" (1917): Readymade urinal
- "Nude Descending a Staircase"
- Questioned art's nature and institution

**Hannah Höch:**
- Political photomontage
- Gender commentary

## Surrealism (1924-1940s)

### Tapping the Unconscious

Based on Freudian psychoanalysis:
- Automatic drawing/writing
- Dream imagery
- Irrational juxtapositions

### Two Approaches

**Automatism:**
- **Joan Miró:** Biomorphic forms
- **André Masson:** Spontaneous gesture

**Veristic Surrealism:**
- **Salvador Dalí:** "The Persistence of Memory"
- **René Magritte:** Philosophical puzzles

## Abstract Expressionism (1940s-1950s)

### New York School

First major American avant-garde:
- Large scale
- Spontaneous execution
- Individual expression
- Action and contemplation

### Two Tendencies

**Action Painting:**
- **Jackson Pollock:** Drip paintings, all-over composition
- **Willem de Kooning:** Gestural brushwork
- Physical act of painting emphasized

**Color Field:**
- **Mark Rothko:** Floating rectangles of color
- **Barnett Newman:** Zip paintings
- Contemplative, spiritual

### Context

- Post-WWII American confidence
- European artists fleeing to New York
- CIA cultural cold war support
- Existentialist philosophy

## Cross-Movement Themes

| Theme | Expression |
|-------|------------|
| Rejection of tradition | All movements |
| Primitive influence | Post-Impressionism, Expressionism, Cubism |
| Unconscious exploration | Surrealism, Abstract Expressionism |
| Technology response | Futurism, Dada |
| War response | Dada, Surrealism, Expressionism |

## Legacy

Modern art established:
- Art as exploration, not decoration
- Multiple simultaneous movements
- Art market as global system
- Criticism and theory as practice
- Avant-garde as cultural role
